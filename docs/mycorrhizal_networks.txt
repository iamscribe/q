The Wood Wide Web: How Mycorrhizal Networks Connect Forests

Beneath every forest floor lies a second forest, invisible and ancient: a labyrinth of fungal filaments so dense that a single cubic centimeter of soil may contain up to 200 meters of hyphae. These are mycorrhizal networks, symbiotic partnerships between fungi and plant roots that have existed for at least 450 million years, predating the evolution of true roots themselves. The term mycorrhiza, coined by Albert Bernhard Frank in 1885, comes from the Greek myco (fungus) and rhiza (root). Today we know that approximately 90 percent of all terrestrial plant species form mycorrhizal associations, and that these fungal networks do far more than help individual plants absorb nutrients. They connect entire forests into communicating, resource-sharing superorganisms.

Mycorrhizal fungi fall into two broad categories based on how they physically interface with plant roots. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, belonging to the phylum Glomeromycota, penetrate the cell walls of root cortex cells and form highly branched structures called arbuscules, which serve as the primary sites of nutrient exchange. These arbuscules have a lifespan of only four to ten days before being digested and reformed. Arbuscular mycorrhizae associate with roughly 80 percent of all plant species, including most grasses, crops, and tropical trees. Ectomycorrhizal fungi, by contrast, do not penetrate cell walls. Instead they form a dense sheath called a mantle around the root tip and extend hyphae between cortical cells, creating an intercellular exchange zone called the Hartig net. Ectomycorrhizal fungi belong primarily to the Basidiomycota and Ascomycota and partner with approximately 2 percent of plant species, but those species dominate temperate and boreal forests: pines, spruces, firs, oaks, birches, beeches, and eucalyptus. A single ectomycorrhizal root tip may be colonized by one fungal species, but a mature Douglas fir can associate with over 100 fungal species simultaneously, with colonization rates reaching 95 percent of available root tips.

The idea that mycorrhizal fungi might link trees into networks rather than serving only as individual partnerships emerged gradually. In 1997, Suzanne Simard, then a doctoral researcher at the University of British Columbia, published a landmark paper in Nature that provided the first field evidence of bidirectional carbon transfer between trees through mycorrhizal networks. Working in the interior forests of British Columbia, Simard grew Douglas fir (Pseudotsuga menziesii) and paper birch (Betula papyrifera) seedlings together in forest soil, then used carbon-13 and carbon-14 isotope tracers to track the movement of photosynthetically fixed carbon. She covered some seedlings with shade cloth to simulate understory conditions. The results were unambiguous: carbon flowed from paper birch to Douglas fir through shared ectomycorrhizal networks, and the transfer increased when the fir was shaded, meaning the birch was effectively subsidizing its neighbor. The net transfer from birch to fir represented roughly 6 percent of the carbon that the fir had fixed through its own photosynthesis. Crucially, when Simard severed the mycorrhizal connections or used fine mesh barriers to prevent hyphal contact, the transfer stopped.

This experiment opened a field. Subsequent work by Simard and colleagues demonstrated that carbon transfer via mycorrhizal networks occurs over distances of up to 30 meters in old-growth forests and that a single large tree can be connected to hundreds of other trees through a shared fungal network. Simard introduced the term "mother tree" to describe large, old, highly connected hub trees that serve as central nodes in these networks. In a 2011 study mapping the network topology of a Douglas fir forest, Simard's team found that the most connected trees, those with the largest diameters and oldest ages, were linked to as many as 47 other trees. The network followed a scale-free architecture, meaning a few hub trees held a disproportionate number of connections, similar to the structure of the internet. Removal of these hub trees, as happens in conventional clear-cutting, caused a cascade of reduced mycorrhizal colonization and increased seedling mortality, with survival rates of regenerating seedlings dropping by 50 percent when mother trees were removed.

The currency of mycorrhizal exchange is not only carbon. Phosphorus is the primary nutrient that arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi provide to their plant hosts. The fungal hyphae, with diameters of only 2 to 10 micrometers, can access soil pores too small for root hairs, which are typically 10 to 20 micrometers in diameter. Ectomycorrhizal fungi are especially adept at mobilizing phosphorus from mineral sources and organic matter through the secretion of organic acids and phosphatase enzymes. In exchange, the plant provides the fungus with photosynthetically fixed carbon, typically in the form of glucose and sucrose. The cost is substantial: plants may allocate 10 to 30 percent of their total photosynthetic carbon to their mycorrhizal partners. Nitrogen also moves through these networks. Ectomycorrhizal fungi can access organic nitrogen directly from decomposing litter through enzymatic breakdown, bypassing the mineralization step that non-mycorrhizal plants depend on. Teste and colleagues at the University of Western Australia demonstrated in 2009 that established mycorrhizal networks could transfer both nitrogen and phosphorus to newly establishing seedlings, providing a significant survival advantage in nutrient-poor soils. Seedlings plugged into existing networks showed 30 percent greater biomass than those without network access.

One of the most provocative discoveries about mycorrhizal networks is their role in defense signaling. In 2010, Yuchun Song and colleagues at South China Agricultural University demonstrated that tomato plants connected by arbuscular mycorrhizal networks of Funneliformis mosseae could transmit defense signals. When one tomato plant was infected with the necrotrophic pathogen Alternaria solani, neighboring plants connected through the mycorrhizal network upregulated defensive enzymes, including peroxidase, polyphenol oxidase, and lipoxygenase, even though they had no direct contact with the pathogen. Unconnected control plants showed no such response. The connected neighbors suffered 50 percent less disease damage when subsequently exposed to the pathogen. Song's team proposed that signaling molecules, possibly jasmonic acid or its derivatives, traveled through the mycorrhizal hyphae.

This finding was extended by Zdenka Babikova and colleagues at the University of Aberdeen in 2013, who used bean plants (Vicia faba) and aphids (Acyrthosiphon pisum) to demonstrate airborne defense signaling mediated by mycorrhizal connections. When aphids attacked one bean plant connected to others through arbuscular mycorrhizal networks, the uninfested connected plants began producing volatile organic compounds that repelled aphids and attracted aphid parasitoids (Aphidius ervi). Plants not connected through mycorrhizal networks did not produce these volatiles. Babikova's experiment was particularly elegant because it used mesh cores to control for diffusion of chemical signals through the soil, isolating the mycorrhizal pathway as the mechanism. The implications are striking: trees and plants may use fungal networks as an underground alarm system, priming their neighbors for attack before the threat arrives.

The mycorrhizal network is not, however, a benevolent commons. Fungi are organisms with their own evolutionary interests, and the relationship involves significant conflict and negotiation. The fungus extracts a carbon toll for its services, and evidence suggests that mycorrhizal fungi preferentially allocate nutrients to host plants that provide more carbon, effectively rewarding generous partners and punishing stingy ones. Toby Kiers and colleagues at Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam demonstrated this reciprocal reward mechanism in 2011, showing that both plants and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi can detect and respond to variation in resource delivery by their partners. Plants allocated more carbon to fungal partners that delivered more phosphorus, and fungi allocated more phosphorus to roots that delivered more carbon. This creates a biological market with supply-and-demand dynamics.

Cheating is also possible. Some plants, known as mycoheterotrophs, have abandoned photosynthesis entirely and parasitize mycorrhizal networks for carbon. The ghost orchid (Epipogium aphyllum) and Indian pipe (Monotropa uniflora) are classic examples. These plants, which lack chlorophyll, tap into ectomycorrhizal networks connecting them to photosynthetic trees and extract carbon without providing anything in return. There are approximately 400 known fully mycoheterotrophic plant species. Partial mycoheterotrophy is even more common: some green orchids and forest understory plants supplement their photosynthetic carbon with carbon extracted from mycorrhizal networks, obtaining 30 to 85 percent of their carbon from fungal sources depending on light conditions.

Parasitic plants can also hijack mycorrhizal networks. The genus Striga, a devastating agricultural parasite in sub-Saharan Africa affecting over 100 million hectares of farmland, uses chemical signals to locate host roots through the mycorrhizal network. Some evidence suggests that arbuscular mycorrhizal networks can actually facilitate the spread of parasitic plant infections by providing physical conduits between host and parasite root systems.

Network architecture matters profoundly for forest resilience. Research by Kevin Beiler and Simard published in 2009 used microsatellite DNA markers to map the genotypes of the ectomycorrhizal fungus Rhizopogon vesiculosus and Rhizopogon vinicolor connecting Douglas fir trees in an old-growth forest near Kamloops, British Columbia. They found that individual fungal genets (genetic individuals) could span up to 20 meters, connecting multiple trees. The largest and oldest trees served as network hubs, each connected to an average of 8 to 12 neighboring trees through shared Rhizopogon genets. Young seedlings establishing near hub trees were significantly more likely to become connected to the network, and connected seedlings had higher survival rates in the critical first years of establishment.

The depth and breadth of resource sharing through these networks challenges classical models of forest ecology built on competition. Simard's research has shown that dying trees increase their carbon transfer to neighbors through mycorrhizal networks, a phenomenon she describes as a legacy effect. In experiments where Douglas fir trees were lethally stressed, the stressed trees ramped up carbon export through their mycorrhizal connections by up to 40 percent, particularly to their own kin. Simard and her PhD student Amanda Asay demonstrated kin recognition in Douglas fir mycorrhizal networks in 2020: mother trees sent significantly more carbon through mycorrhizal networks to their own seedling offspring than to unrelated seedlings, and the kin seedlings showed greater mycorrhizal colonization and higher survival rates.

The agricultural implications are being actively explored. Conventional tillage destroys mycorrhizal networks, and years of heavy phosphorus fertilization can suppress mycorrhizal colonization because plants reduce carbon allocation to fungi when soil nutrients are abundant. Research from the Rodale Institute and others has shown that no-till farming and reduced fertilizer inputs can restore arbuscular mycorrhizal networks within three to five years, with associated improvements in soil structure, water retention, and crop disease resistance. In forestry, Simard has argued that retention of mother trees during logging, keeping at least 30 percent of the largest trees, preserves network integrity and dramatically improves natural regeneration success.

The mechanisms of signal and resource transport through mycorrhizal hyphae remain partly mysterious. Hyphae lack the pressure-driven flow systems of plant vasculature. Transport is thought to occur through a combination of cytoplasmic streaming, vesicle trafficking, and diffusion along concentration gradients. The speed of carbon transfer has been measured at roughly 1 to 2 centimeters per hour through ectomycorrhizal hyphae. Defense signals appear to travel faster, though precise measurements are difficult. Recent work using quantum dot tracers and fluorescent dyes is beginning to visualize the real-time movement of compounds through hyphal networks, revealing that transport is not passive diffusion but involves active cellular mechanisms including motile tubular vacuoles.

The scale of these networks is staggering. A mature forest may contain 200 to 600 kilometers of mycorrhizal hyphae per cubic meter of soil. The total fungal biomass in a temperate forest's soil can exceed 5 tons per hectare, rivaling or exceeding the biomass of all the trees' fine roots. The mycorrhizal network is not a metaphor. It is an anatomical structure as real as the root system or the vascular cambium, and understanding it has become one of the central challenges of 21st-century forest ecology. What Simard calls the "wood wide web" is not a poetic flourish but a description of a physical system through which forests function not as collections of competing individuals but as interconnected communities engaged in complex exchanges of resources, information, and chemical signals. The implications extend from conservation biology to agriculture to our fundamental understanding of what a forest actually is: not a crowd of trees, but a network.
